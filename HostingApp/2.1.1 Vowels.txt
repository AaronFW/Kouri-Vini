2.1.1 Vowels*The following chart presents the Louisiana Creole vowels written using the International Phonetic Alphabet, their graphemes (that is how they are written for Kouri-Vini), and approximations to words in English. Some words–especially those containing [æ], [ø], [œ], [u], and [y]–have alternative pronunciations; in these cases both spellings are acceptable. For example: lœr vs. lèr (‘hour’); astè vs. astœr (“now”).* <table style=width:100%><th>Phoneme</th><th>Grapheme</th><th>Approximations in English</th><tr><td>[ɑ], [a](see Footnote)</td><td>a</td><td>British: b<u>a</u>th, p<u>a</u>ss, tom<u>a</u>to</td></tr><tr><td>[æ]</td><td>æ</td><td>b<u>a</u>t, pl<u>a</u>id, bl<u>a</u>ck</td></tr><tr><td>[ø]</td><td>e</td><td>b<u>u</u>tter, c<u>u</u>t, sh<u>u</u>t, lett<u>u</u>ce</td></tr><tr><td>[e]</td><td>é</td><td>p<u>a</u>ce, b<u>a</u>ke</td></tr><tr><td>[ɛ]</td><td>è</td><td>p<u>e</u>t, s<u>e</u>t</td></tr><tr><td>[i]</td><td>i</td><td>bl<u>ee</u>d, sh<u>ee</u>t, sl<u>ee</u>t</td></tr><tr><td>[ɪ]</td><td>ì</td><td>m<u>i</u>ss, k<u>i</u>ss</td></tr><tr><td>[o]</td><td>o</td><td>c<u>oa</u>t, b<u>oa</u>t, m<u>o</u>st</td></tr><tr><td>[ɔ]</td><td>ò</td><td>b<u>ou</u>ght, s<u>ou</u>ght</td></tr><tr><td>[œ]</td><td>œ</td><td>n/a</td></tr><tr><td>[u]</td><td>ou</td><td>b<u>oo</u>t, l<u>oo</u>p</td></tr><tr><td>[y]</td><td>u</td><td>p<u>ew</u>ter, st<u>ew</u>, m<u>u</u>tilate</td></tr></table>**For diphthongs, where the second vowel is I ([i]), it is spelled with the consonant Y ([j]). *2.1.1.1 Nasal vowels*Louisiana Creole has a set of vowels which are produced by letting air flow out of the nose rather than the mouth. These “nasal vowels” are written as the vowel followed by the consonants N (/n/) or M (/m/). These letters are not pronounced, but signal the nasalization of the vowel.
