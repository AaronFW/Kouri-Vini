4. Capitalization and Hyphenation*Capitalization and hyphenation rules in Louisiana Creole follow Latin language rules.*4.1 Upper-case First Letters*<ul><li>Business names</li><li>Countries</li><li>Given names (first, middle, last names)</li><li>Nationalities</li><li>Proper names</li><li>Street names</li><li>Titles (social, publications, etc.)</li></ul>*4.2 Lower-case First Letters*<ul><li>Adjectives (including in country/place names)</li><li>Nouns (unless the first word of a sentence)</li> </ul>*4.3 Hyphenation*Hyphenation is compulsory where two or more words or names are always uttered together but are not grouped together without spaces or hyphenations. Verbs are exempt from hyphenation.*These generally include the following groups of words:*<ul><li>Animals and insects (e.g. moush-a-fé = firefly)</li><li>Christian saint names (e.g. Sin-Jozèf)</li><li>Countries and other place names (e.g. Léta-Zini)</li><li>Given names (double and triple barrel forenames and surnames)</li><li>Imperatives (e.g. dònn-mò ça = give me that)</li><li>Numbers (cardinal and ordinary)</li><li>Pronouns (postnominal)</li><li>Utensils (e.g. pwal-a-fri = frying pan)</li></ul>*4.3.1 Demonstrative Pronouns*Demonstratives also use hyphenation, and can be either pronominal (especially plural demonstratives in southwest Louisiana) or postnominal.*4.3.1.1 Singular demonstratives*<table style=width:100%><tr><th>This dude</th><th>This cake</th><th>That dude</th><th>That cake</th></tr><tr><td>boug-çila</td><td>gato-çila</td><td>boug-çála</td><td>gato-çála</td></tr></table></body></html>*4.3.1.2 Plural demonstratives*These come in pronominal and postnominal forms.
